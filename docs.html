Landscape Efficiency Frontier Explorer work in progress

An interactive map and AI assistant for exploring the biodiversity · climate · economy trade-offs of any place on Earth — draw an area or name a country, and see where it sits in the three-objective space and how far it is from its efficiency frontier.

About this project

A project of the Eric and Wendy Schmidt Center for Data Science & Environment (DSE) at UC Berkeley.

How much biodiversity or carbon storage must a landscape give up to gain economic value from its land — and how much of that trade-off is actually unavoidable? Polasky et al. 2026 (Science 392:1069, "Landscape efficiency frontiers") showed that for whole nations, large simultaneous gains across all three are often possible: most landscapes sit well inside their frontier, leaving room to improve one objective without sacrificing the others.

That paper published static, national frontiers. This app turns them into a live, any-area, any-weighting exploration. Pick a watershed, a concession, a protected-area buffer, or an entire country; the assistant scores its current biodiversity, irrecoverable carbon, and economic value, then re-solves the land-use optimization on demand so you can feel the trade-offs for the places you care about.

This is a work in progress — layers, objectives, and capabilities are being added and refined.

The efficiency frontier, in one minute

Every parcel of land can be left in its natural state — storing carbon, supporting species — or put to economic use. Across an area, the set of allocations where no objective can improve without another getting worse traces out an efficiency frontier. Where a place falls relative to that frontier is the whole story: a point well inside it means win–wins are still on the table.

Biodiversity

Species richness, threatened-species richness, and range-weighted endemism (IUCN, 2025).

Climate

Irrecoverable carbon — the carbon that, once lost, can't be recovered by 2050.

Economy

Net economic value of production — crops, oil palm, and grazing returns.

Because the optimization is separable — no parcel's best land use depends on its neighbours' — any weighting of the three objectives reduces to an independent per-cell choice, a single database query. That's what lets the assistant re-solve the frontier live as you change the area or the weighting.

📐 About fidelity. The live frontier here is a 2-alternative approximation (keep nature vs. best production). The paper's full result compares 13 land-use alternatives per parcel; that needs the complete transition-cost layers, which are still being ingested. The assistant will tell you when this matters. Biodiversity, carbon, and protected-area layers are our own up-to-date catalog datasets — newer than the paper's frozen inputs — so numbers may differ slightly from the publication.

How it works

This app is powered by GLEN, DSE's open-source framework for AI-assisted geospatial exploration. Open datasets stream straight to your browser from cloud-native formats — no tile server in between — and an AI assistant answers questions by writing live spatial queries against the same data. The frontier optimization never leaves the database: it's plain SQL over an H3 hexagon grid. Full technical details are in the GLEN documentation.

🌱 Low-carbon, open models. Be patient. While much faster than most humans, GLEN makes use of more environmentally-friendly AI models. (Check out the carbon dashboard 🌱 to see the CO₂/energy use of this deployment.)

Exploring the map

The map is built on MapLibre GL — the open-source engine behind the familiar Mapbox-style interactive maps. Scroll or pinch to zoom, drag to pan, and hold Ctrl and drag to tilt and rotate the view. Use the search box to jump to any country, region, or place.

The Overlays panel lists every data layer, grouped into the three Objectives (biodiversity, carbon, economic value), the Constraint (protected areas), Biodiversity Context (ecoregions), and Administrative Boundaries. Toggle a layer on or off with its checkbox, and hover any feature for a tooltip of its live attributes straight from the source data. The objective surfaces the assistant computes — a frontier solution, a win-win mask — are rendered as new hex layers on demand, not pre-baked toggles.

Asking the assistant

The Data Assistant understands plain English and does three kinds of things: it can drive the map, answer analytical questions by running live spatial queries, and draw charts — including the signature frontier scatter. Just type what you want; if it can't do something, it will say so and usually suggest the closest thing it can.

Score and solve for an area:

  • "Show this area's biodiversity, irrecoverable-carbon, and current economic-value scores."
  • "For the area I drew, how much carbon could be gained with no loss of economic value?"
  • "Recolor the map by the balanced-weight optimal land use for Brazil."

Plot the frontier and other charts — results render as a scatter, bar, histogram, or line, not just a table:

  • "Plot Brazil's efficiency frontier: economic value captured vs. biodiversity retained across weightings."
  • "Compare the biodiversity-vs-economy trade-off for Gabon versus Indonesia."
  • "Top 10 countries by irrecoverable carbon outside IUCN I–IV protected areas."

Find win–wins:

  • "Find unprotected cells that are high biodiversity AND high carbon AND low economic value in the Amazon."
✏️ Draw an area to ask about it. Use the draw tool on the map to sketch a polygon — a watershed, a proposed concession, a protected-area buffer, anywhere on Earth — then ask the assistant about it: "Where does the area I drew sit on its efficiency frontier?", "How far inside its frontier is this landscape?" Your drawn shape becomes a live spatial filter for any score, solve, or chart.

Data layers & sources

Every layer draws from a public, primary source. Click "STAC" beside any entry to see the full machine-readable metadata — schemas, column descriptions, license, lineage, and download URLs. The three objectives are aligned on a common H3 resolution-5 grid (~8,000 ha — the paper's parcel size).

Objective: Biodiversity

Species Richness, Threatened Richness & Range-Weighted Richness (IUCN 2025)
Global vertebrate biodiversity rasterized from IUCN range maps (2025): all-vertebrate species richness, threatened-species richness (VU/EN/CR), and range-weighted (endemism) richness. The biodiversity objective takes the max of the three, each min-max normalized — the paper's rule.
STACSource: IUCN Red List
IUCN Red List — spatial data (2025)

Objective: Climate

Irrecoverable Carbon (2024)
Manageable carbon that, if lost, could not be recovered by 2050 — the carbon most worth keeping in the ground. Provided in megagrams of carbon per hectare and summed to the analysis grid.
STAC
Noon et al. 2022 / Conservation International

Objective: Net Economic Value

Crop, Oil-Palm & Grazing Revenue (USD/ha)
Modeled potential economic returns per hectare from the Polasky et al. frontier analysis: current and potential (irrigated / rainfed) crop revenue, current oil-palm revenue, and grazing returns. These density surfaces define the achievable production value for each parcel.
STACSource: Dryad deposit
Polasky et al. 2026 — CC0 (doi:10.5061/dryad.qjq2bvqw5)

Constraint: Protected Areas

World Database on Protected Areas (WDPA)
Global protected-area boundaries with IUCN management categories. Strictly protected areas (IUCN Ia–IV) are treated as a hard constraint — no production is allocated there in the optimization — while categories V–VI are shown for context.
STACSource: Protected Planet
UNEP-WCMC & IUCN — Protected Planet / WDPA

Biodiversity Context

Terrestrial Ecoregions (WWF / Dinerstein 2017)
The world's terrestrial ecoregions, biomes, and realms, with each ecoregion's "Nature Needs Half" protection status (Half Protected → Nature Imperiled) — useful for normalizing biodiversity value regionally and spotting where nature could still recover.
STACSource: Dinerstein et al. 2017
Dinerstein et al. 2017 / RESOLVE Ecoregions

Administrative Boundaries

Countries & Regions / States (Overture 2026)
Overture Maps global administrative divisions — country and region/state boundaries — used to resolve named areas of interest and to summarize frontier metrics by jurisdiction.
STAC (countries)STAC (regions)Source: Overture Maps
Overture Maps Foundation — Divisions (2026-02-18)
